Will polar bears really go extinct by the year 2050?

Some claim that the world's 19 populations of bears are causing their own demise through over-fishing

In the 1960s, there were an estimated 12,000 polar bears in the wild. Today's population has increased to 25,000, so why do some claim that polar bears will be extinct in just a few decades?

The Claim

Polar bears are going extinct due to natural selection, not because of rising global temperatures

The Truth

Polar bears are going extinct due to the dramatic loss of sea ice in their native Arctic habitat, which is caused by warming temperatures

Claim Rating
False
Unclear
True

This claim is false. The world’s polar bear population has increased in the decades since population tracking began, but the numbers don’t paint an accurate picture of the environmental threat they currently face.

Today there are approximately 25,000 polar bears worldwide across 19 populations, with 13 populations living in Canada. In the 1970s there were some 5,000 to 10,000 bears. Their recovery is due to a 1975 treaty that regulates the hunting of polar bears, as well as other conservation efforts. However, the International Union for Conservation of Nature predicts that numbers will decrease by 30 percent or more by the year 2053 due to the loss of the bears’ native ice habitats.

Polar bears spend the winter months on the ice hunting prey and eating their spoils, but when the ice melts each spring they must go onshore where their food options are limited. They are forced to wait until the sea ice refreezes over in the fall and then return to their regular food supply. Rising temperature in the Artic have risen, causing the sea ice to melt sooner and refreeze later, leaving hungry polar bears on land for longer periods.

The polar bear was listed as a species under threat in the 2008 Endangered Species Act. A recent University of Alberta study that tracked the polar bear population in the southern Beaufort Sea from 2001 to 2010 reported that the local bear population declined from 1,500 bears to just 900 bears.

Reports from groups that deny climate change is in part caused by humans include inaccurate information with selectively chosen climate data to paint a false picture about the threat polar bears face. Earlier this year, the U.S. Fish and Wildlife Service released a report stating in certain terms that “the single most important action for the recovery of polar bears is to significantly reduce the present levels of global greenhouse gas emissions.”
